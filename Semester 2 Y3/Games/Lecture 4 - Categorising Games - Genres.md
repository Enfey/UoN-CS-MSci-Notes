## Classifying games
- **Genre** - classification of the game **"system"**
	- The mechanics/core mechanic, core game play loop, interaction patterns, ludological structure(rules and systems) determine a game's system
- When classifying a game, we ask if the *system* matches the pattern of a collection of other games
	- An inexact science, open to debate
		- But this generally aligns with Adam and Rollings' genre taxonomy, which classifies games based on what the player does, and how the rules operate. 
- **Theme** - classification of the aesthetics, story/narrative dressing, and contents
	- We ask how the game is skinned, does it rely on a particular setting?
		- Fantasy, Sci-fi, Western, Realistic

## Action Games
> Real-time, reflex-driven games requiring timing and fast reactions to overcome obstacles

- Emphasies combat, hand-eye co-ordination tests; skill is a primary factor of play
- Sub-genres
	- Shooters
		- First-person shooters
	- Platforms
	- Fighting
- Rules and objects fundamentally describe the game mechanics
	- Lives, energy, score, time limits, collectibles
	![](Pasted%20image%2020260219015536.png)
### Example Action Games
- Counter Strike
	- Shooter
		- Aim -> Shoot -> Avoid Damage -> Survive
		- Economy = buy rounds and rewards dependent on round performance/penalties, utility management
		- Hitscan bullet physics for immediate feedback
		- Progression via eliminating other team
		- Social interaction via teamwork, which facilitates tactical maneuvering
- Mario
	- Platformer
		- Emphasies physics precision
		- Level design as puzzle-space
		- Teaches mechanics implicitly, and has gradual skill escalation, progression mechanic, has power ups for lives. 
- Guilty Gear
	- Frame perfect timing
	- Move lists
	- Spatial control
	- Combos
	- Represent pure competitive mechanical depth, minimal narrative impact on gameplay.
	- Health + rounds as economy, social interaction could even be feinting. 

## Adventure games
> Puzzle-solving through exploration and interaction with people or the environment

- The setting is essential
- Features manipulation of dialogue and objects
- Highly structured narrative, with a more sophisticated emotional tone
- The player is usually represented by someone inside a narrative
- 1st, 2nd, 3rd person
- Often *non-confrontational*
	- Often said to appeal to those who do not normally play video games, not many fail states
- They shift the focus from reflex skill to cognitive skill
- The rules and objects once more drive the games mechancics

## Action adventure
- Combines elements of action and adventure genres
	- Takes exploration and puzzles, and combines with combat and reflexes; almost always weaves in the narrative aspect first and formemost
- Many immediate challenges as found in action games e.g., combat with hand-eye co-ordination
- Long term obstacles and challenges as found in adventure games
	![](Pasted%20image%2020260219025748.png)
- This hybridisation reflects market reality, particularly for AAA games; this genre is the dominant mainstream genre.
### Action Adventure games examples
- Tomb Raider series
- God of War series

## Strategy
> Involve careful planning and resource management to achieve a particular goal/victory

- Many similarities with board games
	- Discrete, turn-based interaction
		- Consistent and balanced rule-set
- Requires careful planning to achieve victory
	- Manage limited set of resources, not necessarily combat; the economy is central and ties in with the degree of tactical maneuvering
	- Offers a 'godlike' view of the game world, indirectly controlling semi-autonomous units
- Theme, simulation, are important as rules and often dictate resources and mechanics
- Diverges into RTS and turnbased TBS.
	![](Pasted%20image%2020260219030655.png)
	

### Examples
- Worms
- Starcraft

## RPG
>Character development through progression
- Inspired by traditional role-playing games such as D&D
- Choose to play one of a number of different characters
	- Dwarf, wizard, thief, corpo
	- High player agency in determining character; want to embrace the roles available to 'play'
	- Focus on gradually developing abilities/'building the character' by completing quests/objectives that reward experience, equipment, abilities etc
	- Highly narrative driven
		- Want to enforce the impression of *Character development*
		- Theme gives the player agency, in tandem with the role, and the available roles are often according to the theme. 
- TBS or real-time combat. 
	![](Pasted%20image%2020260219030649.png)

### Examples
- BG3
- Cyberpunk
- Fallout
- FFXIV.

## Simulation
> Faithful emulation of real-world operating conditions to varying degrees of accuracy.
- There is not necessarily a strong ludological component; 'it is for the sake of doing'
	- This marks differentiation from RTS, where there is competitive context, economic challenges that threaten position/victory, and indirect control
- Here there is direct control
- Mechanics vary by type of simulation entirely
	![](Pasted%20image%2020260219030929.png)
- Simulation focuses on system modelling and the emergent complexity.
